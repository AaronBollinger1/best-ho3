The Electrical Panel Question

Somewhere on every homeowners application is a question about your electrical service — and for a specific list of panels, the answer decides whether a carrier will bind at all. Here's the list, the reasons, and the quote-time math of an upgrade.

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read · Reviewed by a licensed broker, CA DOI Lic. #6013787

What underwriters look at in the garage

Three facts, all readable from the panel door. Type: breakers or fuses — fuse boxes signal pre-modern service and many carriers restrict or require updates. Capacity: 60-amp service, common on older homes, is an application question because modern households load circuits the original design never imagined; 100 amps is the common comfort line, 200 for larger homes. Brand and era: a short list of legacy panels draws specific carrier rules — most famously Federal Pacific (Stab-Lok) and Zinsco.

About Federal Pacific and Zinsco

We'll state only the underwriting fact: many carriers require these panels be replaced before binding, or decline homes that still have them. Whatever the engineering history, the practical consequence for a California homeowner is market access — homes with these panels shop a narrower shelf until the panel changes. Our wizard and every carrier application ask; inspectors photograph the label. Disclose accurately and plan around it.

How panels affect placement, tier by tier

Modern breaker panel, adequate amperage: a non-event — the answer carriers want. Fuse box or 60-amp service: some preferred programs pass; plenty of standard and specialty markets proceed, often with an inspection and sometimes an upgrade timeline. Flagged-brand panels: expect replace-before-bind conditions from much of the admitted market, with E&S as the as-is alternative. The pattern mirrors wiring and roof age: disclosed facts plus a remediation story keep every door from closing at once.

The upgrade, as an insurance project

A panel replacement is among the most contained electrical projects a home can have: no walls open, one permit, done in a day or two in most cases. At quote time it pays in three currencies — eligibility (flagged-panel and fuse-box restrictions disappear), bundling (it's the natural moment to address aluminum terminations or add EV/heat-pump capacity), and narrative (a permitted upgrade with an invoice reads as a maintained home across the whole application). Send your broker the permit and invoice when it's done — mid-term or at renewal, the corrected panel answer reopens markets. The older-home playbook →

Frequently asked

My Federal Pacific panel has worked fine for forty years. Why does anyone care?

Carriers underwrite populations, not anecdotes — and their rules about these panels are about portfolio loss patterns. The rule you experience is simple: many markets want it replaced before they bind. We can quote around it while you decide.

Do I need 200-amp service to get insured?

No — adequacy, not maximalism, is the bar. 100 amps satisfies most carriers for typical homes; the application just wants the true number. Upgrades are about your loads (EVs, ADUs, electrification) as much as underwriting. ADU implications →

Will an upgrade lower my premium?
