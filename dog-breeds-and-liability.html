Dogs and Your Homeowners Policy, Without the Drama

Dog bites are among the most common — and most expensive — homeowners liability claims, so every carrier has a dog policy about dog policies. The approaches differ sharply, nobody's approach is a judgment of your dog, and your job is simpler than the internet suggests: disclose, and shop the carriers whose rules fit your household.

Updated July 2026 · 8 min read · Reviewed by a licensed broker, CA DOI Lic. #6013787

The two underwriting philosophies

Breed lists. Some carriers maintain lists of breeds (and mixes) they won't cover for liability, or won't write at all. The lists vary by company, change over time, and commonly include large guardian and fighting-heritage breeds. We won't reprint anyone's list as fact about dogs — it's a fact about that carrier's appetite, nothing more.

Individual-dog underwriting. Other carriers ask about your dog: bite history, training, how it's contained. A well-mannered dog of any breed sails through; a dog with a bite record faces questions everywhere, regardless of breed.

The practical consequence: a household that gets a "no" from one carrier often gets a routine "yes" from another — the classic reason independent brokers exist.

Why disclosure is non-negotiable

The application asks about dogs; answer it accurately. An undisclosed dog is a misrepresentation sitting quietly in your file until the worst possible moment — a bite claim — when it can poison exactly the coverage you needed. Inspections photograph yards, and adjusters interview neighbors. The disclosed path is boring and safe: the carrier prices the household it's actually insuring, and Coverage E stands behind your dog like it's supposed to. What inspections notice →

If a bite happens

Treat it like the liability event it is: get the person cared for, exchange information, notify your carrier promptly — Coverage F med-pay exists to pay small medical bills fast, no fault admitted, before hard feelings harden. Late-reported bite claims are harder claims. The claim playbook →

When a carrier says no

Options, in the order we try them. A different admitted carrier — philosophies differ; this solves most cases. Liability exclusion for the animal — some carriers will write the home but exclude the dog; sometimes acceptable, always a gap to understand before agreeing. Canine liability policies — standalone animal-liability coverage exists in the specialty market and can backfill an exclusion. E&S placements — the whole household, priced as found. What we don't recommend: quietly rehoming the question. The market genuinely has room for almost every household that tells the truth about itself.

Sizing liability for a dog household

Bite claims are body-injury claims, which means they're priced in medical bills and pain-and-suffering — the expensive kind. Our floor advice firms up around dogs: Coverage E at $300k minimum and a serious look at $500k, plus an umbrella — $1M or more of additional liability for a few hundred dollars a year — when the rest of life (pool, teenagers, visible assets) stacks exposures. Fences, gates, and training aren't just good ownership; they're the underwriting story that keeps options open. The pool version of this page →

Frequently asked

My dog is a mix. How do breed lists apply?

Carrier practices vary — some ask about predominant breed, some about any listed heritage. Answer what you reasonably know (adoption paperwork, DNA test if you have one) and let your broker match the household to the right carrier rather than guessing at the door.

Does my dog raise my premium even with no history?

Usually the dog question is about eligibility and liability terms rather than a line-item surcharge. The honest household with a clean-history dog typically prices normally at the carriers that fit it.

Are guests' dog injuries covered at my house?

That's exactly what Coverage F (small, no-fault) and Coverage E (serious, liability) are for — assuming the dog is disclosed. The theme of this page in one sentence.
