Second and Seasonal Homes: Underwriting the Empty Months

A Tahoe cabin, a Palm Springs getaway, a Central Coast cottage — second homes spend most of their lives unoccupied, often somewhere with real weather and a long fire-department response. That's the whole underwriting story, and it's a manageable one.

Updated July 2026 · 8 min read · Reviewed by a licensed broker, CA DOI Lic. #6013787

What "seasonal" means on an application

Usage is its own question, separate from ownership: primary, secondary/seasonal, or vacant. Our wizard asks it directly because the answer drives the policy. A seasonal home is still furnished and periodically occupied — which keeps it insurable on homeowner-style forms — but carriers underwrite the empty stretches: water damage that runs for days, break-ins with no witnesses, storm damage nobody reports until spring. Expect questions about how often you're there, who checks the house, and what systems watch it while you don't. (A home that's empty and unfurnished is a different animal — see vacancy.)

Water and freeze: the seasonal claim

The signature second-home loss is water — a supply line or heating failure that runs unattended. In the mountains it's freeze: pipes burst during a cold snap and thaw into a flood. Policies commonly expect reasonable care — maintaining heat, or shutting off and draining the system when away in freezing weather — and carriers reward the modern version of diligence: monitored leak sensors, automatic water shutoff valves, and low-temperature alarms that phone you (and a monitoring center) before the ceiling falls. On remote properties these devices aren't gadgets; they're the difference between a nuisance and a rebuild. The device hierarchy →

Mountain & WUI cabins

Many California second homes sit exactly where wildfire appetite is tightest — forested parcels, long access roads, distant hydrants. The same rules apply as anywhere: parcel-level scoring, documented mitigation, and the FAIR + DIC pairing when admitted markets pass. Protection class (fire-department proximity) does real work in the pricing, which is why two cabins an hour apart quote very differently.

Structuring the two-home household

Pair with your primary carrier when you can — some carriers write seasonal homes readily for existing customers, and the umbrella logistics get simpler when one company sees both properties. When the second home's location (brush, protection class) doesn't fit your primary carrier's appetite, split placements through one broker keep the coordination without forcing the fit. Extend the liability picture: your umbrella should schedule the second home, and its underlying-limit requirements apply there too. Contents and toys: furnishings ride on the seasonal policy's Coverage C; boats, ATVs, and golf carts largely don't — they have their own policies, worth one conversation. And if the getaway earns rent when you're not there, that's not a footnote — hosting changes the form and the underwriting: the Airbnb page is required reading, and a dedicated rental belongs on a DP-3.

Frequently asked

Is a second home more expensive to insure than a primary?

Often somewhat, all else equal — unoccupancy and location risk price in. The bigger swings come from the location itself (brush, protection class) and the home's systems, same as any placement. The levers →

Do I need separate liability on the second home?

The seasonal policy carries premises liability; your household's broader liability stacks via the umbrella. What matters is that every property is disclosed to every layer — gaps come from silence, not structure. Coverage E →

What about the weeks family or friends use it?

Occasional personal guests are the normal use case and fine. Regular arrangements that look like tenancy or hosting change the analysis — describe the reality to your broker and let the form follow the facts. Where the line sits →
