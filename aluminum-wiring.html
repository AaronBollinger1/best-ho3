Aluminum Branch Wiring: The 1960s–70s Question

For roughly a decade, copper prices pushed builders to wire homes with aluminum branch circuits. The metal conducts fine; the connection points are the story — and they're why every homeowners application, including ours, asks what's behind your walls.

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read · Reviewed by a licensed broker, CA DOI Lic. #6013787

The era, and whether you're in it

Single-strand aluminum branch wiring — the kind that feeds outlets and switches — appears mainly in homes built or renovated from the mid-1960s through the mid-1970s. That's a large slice of California's suburban stock: early Valley tracts, first-ring Orange County, chunks of the Inland Empire's older neighborhoods. (Large-gauge stranded aluminum feeding a range, dryer, or the main panel is a different, generally unremarkable thing.) If your home is from the era and you don't know, the answer is discoverable: an electrician can identify it quickly, and cable jacket markings often say it outright.

Why the connections are the issue

Aluminum expands and contracts with heat more than copper, oxidizes at exposed surfaces, and creeps under screw-terminal pressure over decades. The cumulative effect concentrates at terminations — outlets, switches, splices — where a loosening connection builds resistance and heat. Underwriters don't debate metallurgy; they read loss data, and the data points at connection points. So the application asks, and carriers sort homes by what's been done about it.

The remediation methods carriers commonly accept

Two families of fixes come up in underwriting conversations: full or partial rewire in copper (the definitive answer), and connection-point remediation — specialized crimp systems (COPALUM-style) or listed aluminum-rated connectors (AlumiConn-style) installed at every termination by a qualified electrician, with documentation. Simple screw-on pigtails with standard wire nuts are the version many carriers do not credit. What matters to your placement: permitted, documented work by an electrician, described accurately on the application. We're reporting the underwriting view, not giving electrical advice — your electrician owns the technical call.

How the market sorts aluminum-wired homes

Roughly three shelves. Remediated and documented — most markets treat the home normally; keep the electrician's invoice and permit forever. Present, disclosed, unremediated — some admitted carriers accept with conditions (an inspection, sometimes a remediation timeline), and specialty/E&S markets price it as found. Undisclosed — the only genuinely bad shelf: discovered at inspection it becomes a scramble, and discovered during a fire claim it becomes a coverage fight. Our wizard flags aluminum answers with a plain-language note and routes accordingly — disclosure is the cheap move.

Sequencing the fix with everything else

If remediation is on your list anyway, sequence it with the projects that share walls and panels: the panel upgrade if a problem panel or 60-amp service is also present, and any planned renovation that opens drywall. On older homes, bundled electrical work is one permit, one story, and one big jump in market access — often timed to a purchase or a pre-renewal season so the improved facts reach underwriters when it counts. Frequently asked

Is aluminum wiring dangerous?

We'll stay in our lane: carriers treat unremediated aluminum branch wiring as elevated risk at the connections, and many require or reward remediation. For safety judgments about your specific home, bring in a licensed electrician.

Will carriers decline me outright?

Some programs pass; enough markets remain that disclosed aluminum homes place routinely. The premium and conditions improve with documentation, and improve most with remediation.

The seller says it was "pigtailed years ago." Good enough?

Get the paperwork. Method matters (listed connectors vs wire nuts), and so does proof. No documentation usually means an electrician's assessment is the next step — worth arranging before escrow closes. The buyer's timeline →
