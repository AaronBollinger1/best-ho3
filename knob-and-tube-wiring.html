Knob & Tube Wiring: The Pre-War Question

If your home predates roughly the Second World War — and much of Los Angeles, the Bay Area, and Sacramento's midtown does — the application's wiring question may be about you. Knob and tube isn't a scandal; it's a material fact carriers price, restrict, or decline on. Here's the full picture.

Updated July 2026 · 8 min read · Reviewed by a licensed broker, CA DOI Lic. #6013787

What knob & tube actually is

Knob-and-tube (K&T) is the original residential wiring method: individual copper conductors running through ceramic knobs and tubes, air-cooled, with no grounding conductor. Installed well, it served for decades — which is exactly the problem, because "decades" have now passed. The rubberized insulation on the conductors grows brittle with age and heat; renovations splice new circuits onto old runs; and blown-in attic insulation buries wiring that was designed to shed heat into open air.

Why underwriters care

Three reasons, all boring and legitimate. No ground — modern appliances and surge conditions assume a grounding path K&T doesn't have. Aged insulation — brittle conductor insulation raises fault risk in exactly the places nobody looks. Concealment — under attic insulation, overheating has nowhere to go and inspection is hard. Carriers respond across a spectrum: some preferred programs require full remediation before binding, others accept homes with limited, inspected K&T remaining, and specialty/E&S markets price it as found. Electrician-certified condition reports move homes along that spectrum.

The disclosure duty

Wiring type is a direct application question — our wizard asks it with a "not sure" option, and "not sure" on a pre-war home means we help you find out. Guessing "copper/modern" on a K&T house isn't optimism; it's a misrepresentation that can surface at inspection or, far worse, during a fire claim. The remediation path

Full rewire is the gold standard: K&T circuits replaced with modern grounded wiring, permitted and inspected, usually alongside a panel upgrade since pre-war service rarely fits modern loads. It's disruptive (walls open) and it's also the single biggest jump an old home's insurability can make in one project. Partial remediation — rewiring accessible areas (attic, basement) and certifying what remains — satisfies some carriers, particularly with a clean electrician's report. Two cautions: splices between old and new wiring are where problems concentrate, so insist on permitted work; and if blown-in insulation is planned for energy reasons, the wiring conversation comes first.

Buying or selling a K&T home

Buyers: get the electrical section of the inspection report early, and get insurance quotes before contingencies lift — wiring is one of the facts that decides which markets will quote at all, and escrow timelines don't enjoy surprises. The buyer's timeline → Sellers: an electrician's certification or a completed rewire is a marketing asset; an undisclosed K&T discovery late in escrow is a renegotiation. Frequently asked

Is knob & tube illegal?

Existing, functioning K&T is generally permitted to remain; the constraints come from insurers and from code requirements when you renovate. We're describing underwriting reality, not giving legal or electrical advice — your electrician and local code authority govern the specifics.

How much does a rewire change my premium?

It varies by carrier and home — the honest headline is market access more than any single discount: full remediation moves a home from restricted markets toward preferred ones, and that jump is usually the money. The levers, ranked →

My K&T was "mostly removed" years ago. What do I tell the wizard?

Pick "mixed" and say so — then let an electrician confirm what remains. Partial truth documented beats confident wrong answers, and carriers have programs for exactly this middle ground.
