The Wildfire Mitigation Checklist

Most homes lost in wildfires ignite from embers — landing in vents, gutters, and mulch — not from a wall of flame. That's good news: ember pathways are exactly the things a homeowner can fix, one weekend at a time. Work the list top to bottom.

Updated July 2026 · 9 min read · Reviewed by a licensed broker, CA DOI Lic. #6013787

The structure itself

☐ Roof — the single biggest factor

A Class A fire-rated roof (composition shingle, tile with birdstops, metal) is the foundation of every hardening story. Wood shake in a brush zone is the opposite. Clear all debris from the roof surface and valleys. How carriers see each roof type →

☐ Vents — ember doorways

Attic, eave, crawlspace, and gable vents are where embers enter. Retrofit with ember-resistant vents or fine metal mesh per current standards. This is one of the cheapest, highest-leverage items on the list.

☐ Eaves, gutters, and edges

Enclosed (boxed) eaves resist ember catch; open eaves collect them. Install gutter guards or keep gutters bare of leaves through fire season. Metal drip edges and bird-stops close small gaps.

☐ Windows and openings

Dual-pane windows (tempered exterior pane where exposed) resist radiant heat far better than single-pane. Weather-seal garage doors — embers ride the gaps.

Zone 0: the first few feet

The area immediately against your walls is the highest-stakes real estate on the property. Carriers and fire scientists now converge on the same guidance: keep it ember-resistant.

☐ Nothing combustible against the house

No bark mulch, no firewood stacks, no stored lumber, no brooms and doormats made of bristle, no junipers hugging the foundation. Hardscape — gravel, pavers, concrete — is the friend of the first few feet. Move firewood and fuel well away from structures.

☐ Decks and attachments

Decks, gates, and fences that touch the house behave like fuses. Screen or enclose the under-deck area and keep it empty; where a wood fence meets the wall, a short metal or masonry section breaks the fuse. Combustible gates directly attached to siding are a classic finding on inspection reports.

Defensible space, working outward

Beyond Zone 0, think in bands that get progressively less strict as they get farther from the structure. Close in: lean, clean, and green — irrigated, maintained plantings with generous spacing, no dead material, tree limbs raised so ground fire can't ladder into canopies. Farther out: thin brush, break up continuous fuel beds, and keep grasses down through the dry months. Slopes deserve extra margin — fire moves faster uphill, so homes above slopes need deeper treated space on the downhill side. Your local fire authority's guidance controls the specifics for your parcel; the underwriting story is the same everywhere: interrupted fuel paths, maintained continuously.

Documentation: the step everyone skips

Undocumented mitigation doesn't exist at quote time. Build the file as you work: dated photos of the roof, vents, Zone 0, and cleared space (retake annually — this is a maintenance record, not a one-time album); receipts and permits for the re-roof, vent retrofit, and tree work; inspection reports from any community program or fire-authority assessment. Send the packet to your broker before remarketing — it feeds carrier questionnaires and Safer from Wildfires credits with participating carriers, and it's your evidence if an aerial image is outdated or wrong.

Frequently asked

Which single item should I do first?

If the roof is sound: vents and Zone 0 — cheap, fast, and directly aimed at ember entry. If the roof is old or wood shake, everything else waits behind it. The roof-age math →

Will this get me a discount?
